Economic Importance Of The Sector

The fishing sector employs nearly 40,000 people and accounts for about 20% of the government's budgetary revenues (estimated in 2016 at 400 million euros). In 2006, all industrial fishing landed in Nouadhibou (northwestern Mauritania) and 80% of artisanal fishing in Nouakchott. According to a 2016 report, 70-80% of the catches in Mauritania's EEZ are not landed in Mauritania but directly abroad (in Las Palmas for example) or transferred to cargo ships to be landed in another country. This generates very little economic and social benefits and does not promote the creation of added value in the fishing sector.

Ressources Fishing And Fishing Types For The Nouadhibou Zone

Zone According to a 2016 report, the dominant fishery resource consists of small pelagic fish (round and flat sardines, sardines, mackerel, mackerel, anchovies, ethmalosis, etc.). Other resources include cephalopods (octopus, cuttlefish, squid), crustaceans (shrimps, lobsters) and demersal fish (dorades, red mullet, soles, etc.). If demersal resources are fully exploited or even overexploited, pelagic resources would presentsignificant operating margins. Two types of fishing are practised in the town of Nouadhibou: industrial fishing and artisanal fishing. Small pelagics make up the dominant catch with about 90% of total catches in quantity. Industrial fishing accounts for 80% of the catch. In 2016, fish processing is low in volume and consists mainly of fish freezing and fishmeal production (approximately 150,000 tonnes per year), an environmentally penalizing and low-value-added activity.

The SMCP

The Mauritanian Fish Marketing Corporation is a mixed-economy company whose shares are 70% owned by the state and 30% by the private sector. Since 5 June 1984, it has been the sole exporter of frozen fish products subject to the landing requirement. It markets about 50,000 tones of frozen fish per year on board and ashore. This is the production of about 74 trawlers, and 46 onshore plants. It has a storage capacity of 19,000 tones. The SMCP aims to ensure the marketing of Mauritanian fish at the best price, to develop added value and to facilitate the integration of fishing income into the national economic cycle. It also aims to withhold tax and piratical taxes, develop national experience in the processing and marketing of fish, and promote and develop small-scale fishing. It helps protect artisanal Mauritanian fishermen from the negative effects of fluctuations in the international fish market. Fisheries Agreements with the European Union Edit The first fisheries agreement between the European Union and Mauritania dates back to 1989. A series of other agreements have since been signed to allow European vessels to fish in Mauritanian waters. It is with Mauritania that the European Union has concluded its largest partnership agreement in the field of sustainable fisheries, an agreement confirmed in July 2015 by a new implementation protocol. In addition to the catches paid, the EU guarantees the country 59.125 million euros, of which 4.125 million euros are directly dedicated to supporting local fishing communities. This agreement is described as exemplary by the European Parliament in its resolution of 12 April 2016. He welcomed the transparency provisions contained in the 2015 Protocol, and called on the Community authorities to draw inspiration from this mechanism in the negotiations they will conduct with other third countries.

The Mauritanian Institute for Ocean and Fisheries Research (IMROP):

This public institution was established in 1978 as the National Centre for Oceanographic and Fisheries Research (CNROP). It takes its current name in 2002 It is responsible for analyzing the biological, physical, socio-economic and technical constraints and determinants of the fishing sector. It conducts assessments of the state of resources, monitoring operating systemsand studies management mechanisms. He is also responsible for the approval of fishing establishments and vessels, the safety of fish products and the conservation of the marine environment. Japanese cooperation enabled it to acquire three oceanographic vessels: N/O N'Diago (1979), L AWAM and AMRIGUE (1997). IMROP conducts more than 8,000 laboratory tests annually in 2003 and issues nearly 5,000 export certificates.
